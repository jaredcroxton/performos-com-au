Getting started with AI for business

26 May 2026 PerformOS Studio ~6 min read

Three moves. No jargon. No vendor lock-in. Everything here applies to an Australian small business that wants to start using AI this quarter, not someday.

Most small business owners know AI matters. They have read the headlines. They have heard the promises. And they have done precisely nothing about it.

Not because they are lazy. Not because they are sceptical. Because nobody has told them what move one actually looks like.

The AI conversation has been dominated by enterprise procurement cycles, six-figure consulting engagements, and breathless product launches that assume you already have a data science team. If you run a business with twelve staff and a genuine desire to get better, almost none of it is written for you.

This article is. Three moves. No jargon. No vendor lock-in. Everything here applies to an Australian small business that wants to start using AI this quarter, not someday.

Move one: Pick one friction, not one instrument

The most common mistake is browsing AI instruments the way you would browse accounting software -- comparing feature lists and pricing tables. That approach misses the point.

AI is not a category of software you buy. It is a capability you apply to a specific piece of friction in your business. The right first question is not "Which AI instrument should I get?" It is "What is the single most repetitive, time-consuming thing we do every week that a machine could handle?"

For most small businesses, the answer sits in one of three places:

  • Customer enquiry handling. You answer the same twenty questions over and over. An AI instrument trained on your business can handle the first response, every time, in seconds.
  • Team knowledge gaps. Your staff ask you the same questions about process, policy, or product. An AI-powered learning instrument gives them answers when they need them, without pulling you out of your work.
  • Reporting and analysis. You spend hours pulling together numbers for a weekly update. An AI instrument can compile, analyse, and surface what changed -- before you sit down to write it.

Notice the pattern. You are not buying AI. You are removing friction that is already costing you time and attention. The instrument follows the problem, not the other way around.

Move two: Start with one person, not the whole team

Rolling out AI to an entire business at once is how projects fail. It is also how they become expensive before they deliver anything.

The operators who get results start small. One person. One workflow. One measurable outcome. Give that person a focused instrument -- something that solves their specific friction -- and let them use it for thirty days.

What happens next matters more than the initial choice of instrument. If the instrument works, that person becomes your internal advocate. They show someone else. Adoption spreads organically because it is spreading through demonstrated value, not a mandate from above. If the instrument does not work, you learn why without having spent on licences for the whole organisation.

The AI Fluency Workshop exists precisely because most small businesses skip this step. They try to solve everything at once, get overwhelmed, and conclude AI is not for them. It is. They just needed a different starting point.

Move three: Measure one number, not a dashboard

The third trap is trying to measure AI impact with a comprehensive set of KPIs before you have even proven the instrument works in your business.

Pick one number. Response time. Hours saved per week. Customer satisfaction score. Enquiries handled without escalation. Whatever matters most to the friction you picked in move one.

Track it manually for the first month. You do not need a dashboard. You need to know whether the thing you bought is doing the job you bought it for. If it is, keep going. If it is not, change the instrument or change the friction you are targeting. Do not change both at once or you will not know which variable moved the needle.

What to ignore

The AI industry is loud. You will encounter people telling you that you need an AI strategy before you do anything else. You do not. You need one friction removed. Strategy follows evidence.

You will encounter vendors selling instruments that claim to do everything. They do not. Instruments that try to solve every problem solve none of them well. Pick the focused one.

You will encounter people who say AI is going to replace your team. It is not. It is going to remove the parts of their job they already find repetitive and frustrating. That makes them better at the parts that need judgement, creativity, and human connection.

The next move

Getting started with AI for business is not about technology. It is about discipline. Pick one friction. Start with one person. Measure one number. Ship the outcome, then repeat.
